🧪 Nerd Zone — How the Sausage Gets Made

The Engine Under the Hood

The Model

Every session you do triggers two competing biological responses: a fitness gain that builds slowly and lasts weeks, and a fatigue cost that hits fast but fades within days. Eric Banister formalised this as the impulse-response model in 1975 — one of sports science’s most battle-tested frameworks.

We track both responses using exponentially weighted moving averages (EMAs). Why EMAs instead of simple rolling windows? Because your body doesn’t forget Tuesday’s session just because Saturday arrived. EMAs give recent sessions more influence while letting older work fade gradually — mimicking how biological adaptation actually works.

The 42-day time constant for fitness reflects how long aerobic and structural adaptations persist. The 7-day constant for fatigue reflects how quickly neuromuscular and glycogen debt dissipates. Both are well-established population averages from Mujika & Busso’s taper research.

Banister et al., 1975. “A systems model of training for athletic performance.” • Mujika & Busso, 1996. Modelling training adaptation.

TRIMP — Quantifying Each Session

TRIMP (TRaining IMPulse) converts every session into a single stress number using your heart rate reserve (%HRR) and duration. Higher-intensity zones carry disproportionate weight because a minute at VO2max hammers you more than a minute of easy jogging.

Zone%HRRWeightEffort feel
Z1< 68%1×Easy / recovery
Z268–82%2×Tempo / steady
Z382–90%3×Threshold / VO2max
Z4> 90%4×Anaerobic / sprint

Zone model inspired by Lucia et al. and the Seiler 3-zone framework.

The Extensions

Fitness (CTL) — Chronic Training Load. A 42-day EMA of daily TRIMP. Think of it as your conditioning bank account — slow to build, slow to drain.

Fatigue (ATL) — Acute Training Load. A 7-day EMA of daily TRIMP. The “I need a nap” signal. It spikes after hard blocks and drops when you rest.

Readiness (TSB) — Training Stress Balance. Fitness minus fatigue. When TSB is positive you’re fresh; when negative you’re carrying load. A taper drives TSB upward before a race.

Load Risk (ACWR)

The Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio divides recent load (ATL) by long-term load (CTL). Maestro uses the uncoupled ACWR model so today’s spike doesn’t contaminate the baseline.

0.8–1.3 is the “sweet spot” where you’re pushing enough to adapt without high injury risk. Above 1.5 you’re in the danger zone — the research shows injury risk roughly doubles.

Gabbett, 2016. “The training—injury prevention paradox.” BJSM.

Monotony & Strain

Monotony (Foster, 1998) = mean daily TRIMP ÷ standard deviation. If every day looks the same, SD → 0, monotony → ∞. High monotony + high volume is the classic recipe for overtraining and illness.

Strain = mean TRIMP × monotony. It captures both how much and how repetitively you’re training. Think of it as a burnout index.

10-day spike compares recent 10-day load to your long-term average. It catches short sharp jumps that the 42-day CTL window might smooth over.

Foster, 1998. “Monitoring training in athletes with reference to overtraining syndrome.” Med Sci Sports Exerc.

TRIMP = duration × %HRR × zone-weight α = 1 − e−1/τ   (τ = 42 for fitness, 7 for fatigue) CTLₜ = CTLₜ₋₁ + α × (TRIMP − CTLₜ₋₁) TSB = CTL − ATL ACWR = ATL ÷ CTL Monotony = mean(daily TRIMP) ÷ SD(daily TRIMP) Strain = mean(TRIMP) × Monotony
